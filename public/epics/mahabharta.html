Indian Origin: Mahabhart (महाभारत)

Mahabhart (महाभारत)

1. Origin

The Mahabhart (महाभारत) is one of the two great Sanskrit epics of ancient India, attributed to the sage Vyasa. It is the longest epic poem in the world, consisting of over 100,000 verses. The Mahabhart tells the story of the rivalry between two families, the Pandavas and the Kauravas, culminating in the great battle of Kurukshetra.

2. Philosophical Foundation

At its core, the Mahabhart deals with complex moral and philosophical issues, including the concepts of duty (Dharma), justice, the consequences of actions (Karma), and the nature of life and death. The Bhagavad Gita, a key philosophical discourse delivered by Krishna to Arjuna, is an integral part of the Mahabhart and provides guidance on the nature of duty, righteousness, and devotion.

3. Key Characters of Mahabhart

  • Krishna: The incarnation of Vishnu, who serves as a guide and friend to the Pandavas, particularly Arjuna. He delivers the Bhagavad Gita.
  • Arjuna: The third Pandava prince, known for his prowess in archery and his moral dilemmas, especially in the battle of Kurukshetra.
  • Yudhishthira: The eldest Pandava, who embodies virtue and dharma, and strives to rule with justice.
  • Draupadi: The shared wife of the Pandavas, known for her devotion and strength in adversity.
  • Duryodhana: The eldest Kaurava, whose ambition and jealousy lead to the great war between the Pandavas and Kauravas.
  • Bheeshma: The grand patriarch of the Kuru family, who plays a critical role in the epic's events.

4. Key Themes

The Mahabhart explores themes such as duty, justice, righteousness, and the moral dilemmas faced by individuals. It emphasizes the consequences of actions and the struggle between good and evil. The epic reflects on the complexity of human nature, the impermanence of life, and the role of fate and destiny in shaping one's journey.

5. Modern Impact

The Mahabhart continues to be a source of inspiration for literature, theatre, film, and television. Its teachings on duty, justice, and morality resonate with audiences across the world. Various adaptations, including TV series and films, have brought the epic to new generations, emphasizing its timeless relevance.
